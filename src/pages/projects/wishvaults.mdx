# Wishvaults

Almost every year, whether it's around a birthday or Christmas time, among my
family and friends, we end up asking each other what we want as gifts. And since
some of us are either terrible at gift-giving or are hard to shop for, a
wishlist tends to be a regular solution. We often ended up sharing Amazon
wishlists or using wishlist apps like Elfster. Personally, I am trying to avoid
Amazon as much as practically possible. And while popular wishlist services can
be convenient, they tend to be clunky and feature bloated while also mining for
our data. This motivated me to build something for my friends and especially my
family to use that is simple, private, and focused on the core intention.

Then, the biggest hurtle and annoyance I find when wanting to try a new service
is the sign up process.

## Goal

The goal of the app is to be intentionally minimal and simple. The main focus is
on the wishlist itself, with a clean and intuitive interface. It should be
simple enough to not require accounts. I would be the sole developer and
maintainer of the service, and I wouln't intend to sell the data. Monetization
of some sort would be nice, but not at the cost of user privacy.

## Tech Stack

I started developing this in mid 2025, and I wanted to finish it by November of
the same year in time for the holiday season. So speed of development was a key
factor. However, I did not want to miss a learning opportunity.

### Backend

For the backend, I chose Scala since it was the language I had been working on
for the past few years and I wanted to continue improving my skills in it. Also,
while I had been using Typelevel libraries for a while, I had not yet built a
full application with them, so I chose cats-effect, http4s, doobie, and circe
for the backend. I also used Postgres as the database since I am familiar with
it and it is a reasonable choice for the relational data models of this
application. I had used Flyway years ago for database migrations and I have so
far preferred its simplicity and straightforwardness over other more complex
tools I had been using in professional settings.

### Frontend

I wanted to use this side of the application to refresh my frontend skills after
performing poorly in a job interview that had a heavy frontend component and I
approached it with a little too much confidence. Gatsby would be the framework
to build with to get a static site with good performance and SEO just in case I
wanted to make the app public. For styling, I went with styled-components for
the sake of experiencing "plain CSS". I had been using Tailwind in the past and
it did feel as if my CSS skills had been getting a little rusty.
